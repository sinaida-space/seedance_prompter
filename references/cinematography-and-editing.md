# Cinematography & Editing Theory

Framing, blocking, and composition vocabulary for building beats and character
placement — plus the editing theory that governs how *multiple* Seedance renders
(a shot list) should be sequenced and cut together. Pull concrete terms from here the
same way you pull from `camera-and-style.md` — never invent vaguer equivalents.

## Shot compositions (framing types)

### By subject size in frame

| Term | What it shows |
|---|---|
| Extreme Wide Shot (EWS) | establishes location; subject tiny or absent |
| Wide Shot (WS) / Long Shot (LS) | full body of the subject |
| Full Shot (FS) | entire subject head to toe, often centered |
| Medium Long Shot (MLS) | from the knees up — the "cowboy shot" |
| Medium Shot (MS) | waist up; emphasizes body language |
| Medium Close-Up (MCU) | chest or shoulders up; blends intimacy and context |
| Close-Up (CU) | just the face or object; reveals emotion |
| Extreme Close-Up (ECU) | one facial feature or object detail |

Note the moderation guidance elsewhere in this skill: ECU/CU on a real identity
reference raises rejection risk — prefer MS/MCU when identity lock matters, save
ECU for objects or a locked, already-established character.

### By camera angle

| Term | Effect |
|---|---|
| Eye-Level | neutral, natural |
| High Angle | camera looks down; subject reads small or weak |
| Low Angle | camera looks up; subject reads powerful or intimidating |
| Overhead / Bird's Eye | directly above; abstract or strategic feel |
| Worm's Eye View | extreme low angle; dramatic or surreal |
| Dutch Angle (tilted) | diagonal horizon; unease, tension, disorientation |
| Over-the-Shoulder (OTS) | from behind a character's shoulder; dialogue coverage |
| Point-of-View (POV) | as if through a character's eyes |
| Tracking / Following Shot | camera follows a character's movement |

## Blocking techniques (actor/subject placement & movement)

Use these when a beat needs more than "camera moves, subject does X" — blocking is
*where* the subject sits in the frame and *how* they relate to other subjects or
depth, and it's a cheap way to make a static-camera beat still read as directed.

### Static blocking

- **Centered blocking** — subject center-frame; control or focus
- **Rule of thirds** — subject at 1/3 of frame; balanced, the default choice absent a reason to deviate
- **Foreground/background interaction** — one subject close to lens, another far; depth or power dynamic
- **Symmetrical blocking** — mirrored subjects/elements; formal, stylized (Wes Anderson register)
- **Isolated blocking** — subject alone in a wide shot; solitude, vulnerability

### Dynamic blocking

- **Crossing paths** — subjects move past each other; conflict, missed connection
- **Overlapping movement** — one subject moves into/out of another's space; intimacy or tension
- **Depth blocking** — subjects at varying distances from camera; hierarchy, emotional distance
- **Circular blocking** — subjects orbit each other; shifting power, negotiation
- **Triangular blocking** — three-subject layout; love triangles, power struggles

### Movement-based blocking

- **Walk and talk** — subjects converse while moving; energetic, expository
- **Push in / pull out** — camera moves toward/away while the subject stays still
- **Switching focus** — attention shifts between subjects without moving the camera (pair with rack focus, below)
- **Tableau blocking** — subjects hold a fixed pose, painting-like; dramatic, formal
- **Re-blocking** — subjects reposition mid-take; shifts tone, power, or emphasis — powerful but expensive in a 15s render, use sparingly

## Advanced composition techniques

### Framing devices
- **Frame within a frame** — doors, windows, mirrors isolating or spotlighting the subject
- **Silhouetting** — subject in shadow against a lit background; emphasizes form over detail
- **Reflections** — mirrors, puddles, glass for thematic layering

### Lens effects
- **Deep focus** — everything foreground-to-background in focus (Citizen Kane register)
- **Shallow focus** — one plane sharp, rest blurred; directs viewer attention
- **Rack focus** — focus shifts from one subject to another mid-shot — write as its own beat, camera-first phrasing still applies: `[Xs–Ys] Rack focus from [A] to [B]. [what happens].`

### Perspective & line
- **Leading lines** — roads, hallways, beams guiding the eye toward the subject
- **Diagonals** — angled elements create dynamic tension
- **Negative space** — empty area around the subject; isolation, vulnerability

## Shot function (narrative purpose)

Use these to decide what each beat — or each shot in a multi-render shot list — is
*for*, not just what it looks like:

- **Establishing shot** — sets the scene (often the opening beat)
- **Master shot** — covers the entire scene/action in one continuous take
- **Insert shot** — focus on one object or detail
- **Reaction shot** — shows a character's response to something (see Murch below — this is usually where a cut belongs)
- **Cutaway** — briefly shifts to something outside the main action
- **Two-shot / group shot** — two or more subjects in frame, for dialogue or relationship
- **Shot/reverse shot** — alternates between subjects during a conversation
- **Montage** — a series of shots conveying passage of time or a thematic connection

## Editing theory (for sequencing a shot list)

A single 15-second render only needs beats. A **shot list** — several renders cut
together into one longer piece — needs an actual theory of *where the cuts go and
why*. Use the two frameworks below when planning shot-list transitions, deciding
where one render should end and the next begin, and choosing what a reaction/cutaway
beat is for.

### Walter Murch's Rule of Six (priority order for where a cut belongs)

From Murch's *In the Blink of an Eye* — when deciding where a cut should fall,
weigh these in order, most important first. A cut that's rhythmically perfect but
emotionally wrong is still a bad cut.

1. **Emotion (51%)** — does the cut preserve or advance what the audience *feels*
   at that moment? This outweighs everything below it combined.
2. **Story (23%)** — does the cut move the story forward?
3. **Rhythm (10%)** — does the cut land at a rhythmically satisfying moment?
4. **Eye-trace (7%)** — does the cut respect where the viewer's attention already is
   in the frame, so the next shot doesn't force a jarring re-search?
5. **Two-dimensional plane of screen (5%)** — is the cut compositionally coherent
   left-right/up-down across the edit?
6. **Three-dimensional space of continuity (4%)** — does the cut respect physical
   continuity (eyeline, screen direction, the 180° line)?

The ranking is deliberate: continuity — the thing most editing advice obsesses
over — is Murch's *least* important criterion. A cut that breaks the 180° line but
nails the emotional beat still works; a technically continuous cut that kills the
emotional read does not.

**Practical use in a shot list:** when choosing where render N ends and render N+1
begins, first ask what the audience should *feel* at that boundary — that decides
the cut point before camera angle or rhythm even enter the conversation.

**Murch's blink observation:** people blink involuntarily at the moment they finish
processing a thought — not randomly. In a two-person conversation, the *listener*
blinks right when they've absorbed what was said; that's the natural place to cut to
their reaction. When writing a reaction-shot beat, put it at the point where the
other subject would have just finished registering what happened — not a beat
earlier or later. This is also why an editor "thinking hard" or scanning rapidly
through unrelated ideas blinks more often and faster — rapid blinking reads as
active, effortful cognition; a held, unblinking beat reads as arrested attention or
shock. Use that deliberately: a beat that should read as the character being
stopped in their tracks should specify *no blink*, held gaze.

Two related ideas from the same theory, useful when writing the connective tissue
between beats in a shot list:
- **Coherent hearing** — the audience should always be able to construct one
  coherent internal "soundtrack" of intent across a cut, even in silence; a cut that
  makes the viewer briefly lose track of why the last thing just happened breaks
  this.
- **Connected consciousness** — a well-cut sequence reads as one continuous
  train of thought shared between camera and audience, not as a series of separate
  observations. Aim beats at this rather than at pure visual variety.

### The Kuleshov Effect (montage / juxtaposition)

Lev Kuleshov's 1910s-20s experiment (Moscow State Film School) intercut the *same*
neutral shot of an actor's face with three different follow-up images — a bowl of
soup, a dead woman in a coffin, a girl playing. Audiences read the identical face as
hungry, grieving, or delighted, purely from what it was cut against. The actor's
performance never changed; the meaning came entirely from the juxtaposition.

This is the foundational proof that **meaning in film lives in the cut, not just in
the shot** — Kuleshov's students Pudovkin and Eisenstein built Soviet montage theory
on top of it, with Eisenstein later formalizing five montage methods (metric,
rhythmic, tonal, overtonal, intellectual — worth a deeper look only if a project
calls for explicitly dialectical/associative editing, e.g. a symbolic or political
piece).

**Practical use for this skill:**
- Since Seedance renders one shot at a time, the Kuleshov effect is exactly the tool
  for a **shot list**: a neutral or ambiguous reaction beat (a face giving nothing
  away) can be made to mean entirely different things depending on what it's placed
  next to in the edit. Write the reaction shot itself as genuinely neutral — let the
  juxtaposition, not the performance, carry the meaning.
- Applies directly to **insert shots and cutaways**: what an insert shot *means*
  (threat, desire, memory, dread) is set by its position in the sequence, not by
  making the insert itself more expressive. An insert of a locked door only reads as
  "trapped" if it's placed against the right adjacent beat.
- When diagnosing a shot list that "feels flat" even though every individual render
  looks good, the fix is often not a new shot — it's re-ordering or re-pairing the
  existing ones so the Kuleshov juxtaposition does the emotional work.
